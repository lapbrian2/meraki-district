The question of who qualifies as a creator has always been political. For centuries, the answer was determined by institutions: academies, galleries, publishers, studios. You were a creator if they said you were. Your work existed if they showed it.

That system had a clear logic and a clear cost. The logic was curation. The cost was exclusion. Entire categories of practice were invisible because the gatekeepers did not recognise them.

## The access era

AI tools and digital platforms dismantled those gates. Anyone can publish. Anyone can generate. Anyone can call themselves a creator and share work with an audience of millions.

This is genuinely good. The democratisation of creative tools has surfaced extraordinary practitioners who would have been invisible under the old system. It has also surfaced an enormous volume of work that is technically produced and creatively indifferent.

The old system filtered too aggressively. The current system does not filter at all. Neither extreme serves creators well.

## The credential gap

What is missing is a credentialing layer that measures demonstrated practice rather than institutional proximity. A system where recognition comes from doing the work consistently and well, evaluated by practitioners rather than administrators.

This is what The Seal is designed to address. Four tiers of recognition, each requiring specific evidence of practice: a body of work, peer evaluation, contribution to the creative community, and sustained development over time.

The point is not to recreate the gatekeeping of traditional institutions. The point is to create a signal that means something in a landscape where the noise is overwhelming.

## Earned recognition

A credential that can be purchased is not a credential. It is a transaction. A credential that is given freely is not a credential. It is a courtesy. A credential that requires demonstrated practice, peer review, and real contribution is something else entirely. It is evidence.

The question of who gets to call themselves a creator has a simple answer: anyone who does the work. The question of who is recognised for that work is more complex, and it is the question we are trying to answer honestly.

We believe recognition should follow practice. It should be transparent in its criteria, rigorous in its evaluation, and available to anyone willing to meet the standard. Not because they attended the right school or know the right people, but because the work speaks.

That is what earned recognition looks like. We are building the infrastructure to make it possible.
