The prevailing wisdom says share everything. Document the journey. Show the messy middle. Build in public and let the audience participate in the process. There is real value in this approach. It builds trust. It creates accountability. It turns the lonely work of creation into something communal.

## But there is a cost

When every sketch is a potential post and every draft is content waiting to happen, something shifts in the creative process itself. You begin to optimise for shareability before the work has had time to become itself. The rough edges that might have led somewhere interesting get smoothed away too early, not because they were wrong but because they were hard to explain in a caption.

## The case for strategic privacy

The most interesting creative work often comes from periods of deliberate obscurity. Not secrecy—obscurity. The difference matters. Secrecy is about hiding. Obscurity is about protecting space for the work to develop without the pressure of an audience.

Some of the best studios in the world operate this way. They share the finished work generously, but the process stays inside the room. Not because they are precious about it, but because they understand that creative thinking is fragile at certain stages. It needs time to be wrong before it can be right.

## Finding your own line

This is not an argument against transparency. It is an argument for intentionality. Know what you are sharing and why. Understand the difference between generosity and performance. Some work gains power from being shared early. Other work gains power from arriving fully formed, without explanation, carrying the quiet authority of something that was allowed to become what it needed to be.

The question is not whether to build in public. The question is: which parts of the building deserve privacy?
