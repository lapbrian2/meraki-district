There is a growing gap between what AI can produce and what a practiced creator can make. Not because the tools are lacking—they are extraordinary—but because the gap was never about capability. It was always about intent.

## The market is splitting

We are watching the creative economy bifurcate in real time. On one side: an ocean of competent, frictionless, algorithmically optimised output. On the other: work that carries the weight of a human being who chose every detail deliberately. The first category is getting cheaper by the month. The second is getting more valuable by the day.

This is the craft premium. It is the delta between what a machine can approximate and what a person with taste, experience, and conviction can create. It is not nostalgia for handmade work. It is a recognition that certain qualities—judgement, restraint, the decision to leave something out—cannot be automated because they are not skills. They are positions.

## What the premium actually requires

Claiming the craft premium is not as simple as adding "human-made" to your bio. The market will not take your word for it. You need to demonstrate the difference in every detail: the typography you chose instead of accepted, the paragraph you cut instead of kept, the reference you made that only someone steeped in the work would recognise.

The craft premium is earned in the margins. It lives in the decisions that no one asked you to make but that everyone can feel.

## Where this leads

The creators who understand this shift are not fighting AI. They are using it—strategically, selectively, as one instrument among many. But they are also doing something AI cannot: they are developing a point of view. And in a market flooded with competent neutrality, a genuine point of view is the scarcest resource there is.
